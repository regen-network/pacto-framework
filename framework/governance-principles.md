# Governance Principles: Implementing Rights Through Participatory Practice

PACTO provides participatory methodology for implementing the rights of indigenous peoples and local communities to self-determination, territorial governance, and meaningful participation in decisions affecting their lands and livelihoods. These rights are established in international law — UNDRIP, ILO Convention 169, and regional jurisprudence including Sarayaku v. Ecuador (IACtHR, 2012) — and in national constitutions such as Ecuador's Articles 57, 71, and 398. PACTO does not create these rights. It provides tools and methodology for their full exercise.

---

## Rights Foundation

PACTO's governance principles are grounded in pre-existing legal instruments. The table below identifies the key provisions and PACTO's relationship to each.

| Legal Instrument | Key Provisions | PACTO's Relationship |
|-----------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **UNDRIP** (2007) | Art. 3 (self-determination), Art. 18 (participation in decision-making), Art. 19 (FPIC), Art. 26 (land rights), Art. 31 (cultural heritage & traditional knowledge) | PACTO provides methodology for exercising these rights in ecological governance contexts |
| **ILO Convention 169** (1989) | Art. 4 (special measures), Art. 6 (consultation in good faith), Art. 7 (right to decide development priorities), Art. 15 (natural resource rights) | PACTO instruments the "good faith" and "culturally appropriate" consultation requirements with structured participatory process |
| **Sarayaku v. Ecuador** (IACtHR, 2012) | Consultation must be (1) prior, (2) culturally appropriate, (3) in good faith, (4) oriented toward consent | PACTO's Core Loop operationalizes each of these requirements through voice-first, iterative, community-controlled process |
| **Ecuador Constitution** (2008) | Art. 57 (collective rights), Art. 71 (Rights of Nature), Art. 398 (environmental consultation) | PACTO supports communities in exercising constitutional collective rights through documented, verifiable participatory process |

## Core Principle: Realizing the Full Intent of Consent

The right to Free, Prior, and Informed Consent is well established. The problem is implementation. In practice, FPIC is often reduced to a procedural minimum — a signature on a consent form for terms drafted externally — rather than the substantive participatory right that international law intends.

PACTO addresses this implementation gap. It does not propose an alternative to FPIC; it provides methodology for implementing FPIC at the depth these legal instruments require.

| Dimension | Minimal FPIC Implementation (common practice) | Full Rights Implementation (what PACTO methodology supports) |
|-----------|-----------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Direction** | External to Community | Community to External |
| **Community role** | Reviewer | Author |
| **Authority over terms** | Limited (accept/reject) | Full (define/modify) |
| **Temporal scope** | Point-in-time consent | Ongoing co-design |
| **Evidence** | Consent documented | Entire process documented |
| **Amendment** | Renegotiation required | Living contract protocol |

## What PACTO Is Not

1. **PACTO is not an alternative to FPIC.** FPIC is a right. PACTO is a methodology for implementing that right. They are not substitutes.
2. **PACTO does not create a new standard for consent.** The standards exist in UNDRIP, ILO 169, and regional jurisprudence. PACTO provides tools for meeting them.
3. **PACTO is not required for a community's governance to be legitimate.** Communities governed themselves long before PACTO and will continue to do so without it. PACTO is an optional methodology that communities may choose to use.
4. **PACTO does not grant communities their voice.** Communities have always had voice, authority, and governance. PACTO provides technology that makes existing governance processes more legible to external parties — funders, regulators, and verifiers.
5. **PACTO does not define "biocultural."** Biocultural values are defined by communities through their own cultural frameworks. PACTO documents and structures these values for use in ecological agreements.

## Assembly Protocols

### Respect Existing Governance

PACTO does not introduce new governance structures. It instruments existing ones — indigenous assemblies, tribal councils, community meetings, or any locally recognized decision-making forum.

The community decides: when to convene, who participates, what governance rules apply, what constitutes a quorum, how decisions are ratified.

### Voice-First Participation

Oral participation is the primary interface. PACTO accommodates:

- **Oral-only participants** — community members who cannot read or write participate through voice, with equal weight
- **Multi-language deliberation** — internal discussion in community language, formal capture in administrative language
- **Visual and symbolic expression** — maps, drawings, gestures as valid input
- **Elder authority** — traditional knowledge holders participate through oral traditions and cultural protocols

### Community Authority at Every Step

| Core Loop Step | Community Authority |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Assembly | Community convenes on its own terms |
| Voice Capture | Community approves capture methods and data handling |
| Synthesis | AI output is provisional; community word is final |
| Feedback | Community corrects, affirms, or rejects synthesis |
| Refinement | Community sets consensus threshold and iteration rules |
| Agreement | Community endorses final terms; can withdraw at any time |

---

## Data Sovereignty

1. **Community ownership** — assembly recordings, transcripts, and synthesis outputs belong to the community
2. **Community-defined access** — the community determines who can access their data
3. **Right to withdraw** — communities can request removal of their content at any time
4. **Storage self-determination** — communities choose where their data is stored
5. **No extraction without reciprocity** — every use of community data must add value for the community

### Rights Grounding for Data Sovereignty

These commitments are not PACTO inventions — they implement specific provisions of existing rights instruments:

- **UNDRIP Article 31** — indigenous peoples have the right to maintain, control, protect, and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural expressions
- **ILO Convention 169 Article 4** — special measures shall be adopted to safeguard the persons, institutions, property, labour, cultures, and environment of indigenous peoples
- **Ecuador Constitution Article 57(12)** — the right to maintain, protect, and develop collective knowledge; their sciences, technologies, and ancestral wisdom

## Community Rights and Process Integrity Checklist

For any work involving indigenous knowledge, community processes, or pilot-specific content:

- [ ] **Community authorship** — was this generated through a PACTO process?
- [ ] **Community endorsement** — has the community reviewed and affirmed it?
- [ ] **Data sovereignty** — does it respect the community's data requirements?
- [ ] **Attribution** — is the contributing community properly credited?
- [ ] **No extraction** — does it add value for the community?
- [ ] **Language integrity** — has translation been verified by a community member?
- [ ] **Power dynamics** — does it risk creating dependency or externally-imposed frameworks?
- [ ] **Reversibility** — can the community withdraw this content?
- [ ] **Rights grounding** — does it reference the established legal foundation rather than framing PACTO as the source of rights?

## Legal Pluralism

PACTO-generated agreements may need to be simultaneously valid under multiple legal frameworks:

| Legal Framework | Scope |
|----------------|-------|
| **Indigenous customary law** | Community governance, traditional authority |
| **National law** | State recognition, contract enforcement |
| **On-chain governance** | Ledger state, smart contract execution |
| **International frameworks** | UNDRIP, ILO Convention 169, CBD, TNFD, IACtHR jurisprudence |

PACTO does not attempt to resolve legal pluralism in the abstract — it provides the process for communities to navigate it with appropriate legal support.

## Power Dynamics Safeguards

The use of AI in indigenous community processes carries specific risks. PACTO mitigates them through:

- **Community override** at every AI-mediated step
- **Community endorsement** required before any knowledge is published
- **Indigenous language preservation** — translations flagged for community review
- **AI synthesis is always provisional** — community word is final
- **Voice-first design** — no requirement for digital literacy
- **Depth of deliberation serves community goals** — AI-augmented synthesis makes deliberation more accessible, not faster

## Co-governance

PACTO is not developed in isolation and imposed on communities. It is developed through co-governance with the communities and partners who use it.

- **Methodology evolution** — PACTO's Core Loop and governance principles evolve based on what communities and implementation partners learn through practice
- **Partner voice** — implementation partners (facilitators, indigenous rights organizations, legal counsel) contribute to PACTO's development based on their field experience
- **Community veto** — communities have the right to reject any aspect of PACTO methodology that does not serve their needs, and to adapt the methodology to their governance context
- **Trajectory toward indigenous ownership** — the long-term goal is for PACTO infrastructure — tooling, data storage, synthesis systems — to be owned and operated by indigenous communities and their organizations, not by external technology providers
- **Shared learning** — insights from PACTO processes are shared across the network (with community consent) to strengthen the methodology for all participants
